# Complaint to the Financial Regulators Complaints Commissioner
## Regarding the Financial Services Authority's Failure to Act on Intelligence Relating to LIBOR Manipulation

---

**Complainant:** Ewan Bramley
**Address:** 204 Jesmond Dene Road, Jesmond, Newcastle upon Tyne
**Date:** 21 February 2026
**Reference:** Complaint under Section 84, Financial Services Act 2012

---

## OPENING STATEMENT

I am Ewan Bramley, a UK citizen and business owner. I operated a limited company from 2009 to 2023, during which time I held commercial borrowing with RBS — one of the sixteen LIBOR panel banks subsequently fined $612 million for benchmark manipulation.

I am not seeking compensation. I am not claiming personal loss. I am filing this complaint because the questions it contains have never been formally asked and answered in the public record, and I believe they should be.

The Financial Services Authority received formal intelligence from United States regulatory authorities about LIBOR manipulation. It did not act. Its own internal audit subsequently confirmed that it had received 74 relevant communications — 26 of them directly referencing deliberate rate suppression — and failed to escalate them. The Treasury Select Committee found the FSA was two years behind its US counterpart in opening a formal investigation.

None of the individuals responsible for that failure have been asked to formally account for it. The Commissioner is the appropriate body to establish what obligations existed, whether they were met, and what the public record should show.

That is all I am asking for.

## Preliminary Note on Process

This complaint is made under the Financial Services Act 2012, Section 84, which establishes the Financial Regulators Complaints Commissioner and provides that any person may complain about the way the Financial Conduct Authority (and its predecessor, the Financial Services Authority) exercised — or **failed to exercise** — its regulatory functions.

This complaint concerns the FSA's failure to exercise its regulatory functions in response to intelligence it received between 2007 and 2009 regarding LIBOR manipulation.

---

## PART ONE: THE FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### 1.1 What LIBOR Was

The London Interbank Offered Rate was the benchmark that priced approximately $350 trillion in financial contracts globally. In the United Kingdom it directly priced mortgages, variable-rate business loans, student loans, and structured financial products. Approximately thirty million UK mortgage holders were affected by movements in this benchmark.

It was calculated through a daily submission process: sixteen major banks submitted their estimate of their own borrowing costs. An administrator stripped the highest and lowest, averaged the rest, and published a single number. That number moved through the global financial system and into the monthly mortgage payments of millions of UK households.

### 1.2 The Governance Structure and Its Conflict

The British Bankers' Association's Foreign Exchange and Money Markets Committee held sole responsibility for "all aspects of the functioning and development of LIBOR." That committee was chaired by a representative of one of the submitting panel banks. The same banks submitting the rate also populated and chaired the body responsible for overseeing it.

In September 2012, Martin Wheatley — commissioned by HM Treasury to review LIBOR — described this as creating "a conflict of interest that precludes strong and credible governance." The BBA declined to publicly identify committee members, stating it wanted to protect them "from being lobbied."

### 1.3 What the FSA Knew, and When

This is the central matter of this complaint.

**2005:** John Ewan, the BBA Director responsible for LIBOR, wrote internally that there was "consensus amongst banks that sterling LIBOR and US dollar are being set 3–4 basis points above the true cash rate." This is a documented, dated, internal written record from the individual whose role was to run the benchmark, recording that the benchmark was wrong. Seven years before the public scandal broke.

**2007–2008:** The FSA's own internal audit, commissioned and published in March 2013, reviewed communications from January 2007 to May 2009 and found:

- **74 communications** in which FSA staff had received information touching on LIBOR concerns
- **26 of those contained direct references to lowballing** — banks deliberately underreporting their LIBOR submissions to disguise their funding distress during the financial crisis
- The majority of direct lowballing references were concentrated in **April to June 2008**

The audit's conclusion was unambiguous: the FSA "should have made sure that communications were better analysed, circulated and escalated in an appropriately targeted manner." The audit attributed the failure to "tunnel vision" — the FSA was "too narrowly focused in its handling of LIBOR-related information" during the financial crisis.

**The FSA was formally notified by US regulatory authorities.** The Treasury Select Committee's report "Fixing LIBOR: Some Preliminary Findings" (HC 481, August 2012) established that the FSA was **two years behind the United States Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) in opening a formal LIBOR investigation**. The CFTC operates under a formal Memorandum of Understanding with the FSA/FCA for the sharing of regulatory intelligence. Intelligence about potential manipulation of a global interest rate benchmark affecting millions of British borrowers was shared through this formal channel. The FSA received it. The FSA did not act.

## PART TWO: THE SPECIFIC REGULATORY FAILURES

### 2.1 The Power That Was Not Exercised

Contributing to LIBOR was not a "regulated activity" under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000. The FSA correctly notes this as a jurisdictional constraint. However, this does not explain or excuse the failure for the following reasons:

**First, the FSA had powers it could have exercised.** Under **Section 165 FSMA 2000**, the FSA could at any time require an authorised firm to provide information. By April-June 2008, 26 documented internal communications directly referenced lowballing by regulated banks. The threshold for a Section 165 information-gathering exercise is not proof of a breach — it is reasonable grounds to seek information. That threshold was met. The power was not used.

**Second, Section 168 FSMA 2000** permitted the FSA to appoint investigators where it had reasonable grounds to suspect market manipulation or misleading practices by an authorised person. At the point where 26 direct lowballing references had accumulated — particularly given the formal intelligence received from the CFTC — reasonable grounds existed. The power was not used.

**Third, FSA Principle 11.** The FSA's Principles for Businesses state that a regulated firm must "deal with [its] regulators in an open and cooperative way" and "disclose to the FCA appropriately anything relating to the firm of which the FCA would reasonably expect notice." When Barclays staff told FSA supervisors in 2007 and 2008 that LIBOR submissions were not reflecting actual market rates, this was a Principle 11 disclosure. A Principle 11 disclosure creates a downstream obligation. The FSA did not characterise it as such and did not act on it as such.

### 2.2 The Asymmetry in Mandatory Reporting Obligations

There is a structural point that this complaint places directly before the Commissioner.

Under **SUP 15.3 of the FCA Handbook**, every regulated firm is required to notify the FCA promptly when it becomes aware of anything that the FCA "would reasonably expect notice" of — including anything that could constitute market manipulation. There is no discretion at the firm level: the obligation to notify is mandatory once the threshold is met.

There is no equivalent mandatory escalation obligation binding on FSA staff when they receive such information from the regulated sector. The 74 communications — including 26 direct lowballing references — landed with FSA supervisors who were under no statutory duty to aggregate them, escalate them, or formally document their decision not to act. The FSA required of the firms it regulated a standard it did not apply to itself.

### 2.3 The Intelligence from Allied Regulators

This complaint asks the Commissioner to address a specific and serious question that has not been answered publicly.

A mortgage holder who contacts the FSA to raise a concern about their bank is a member of the public raising an individual complaint. That complaint enters a queue. It is logged, given a reference number, managed through a process designed for volume.

A formal notification from the United States Commodity Futures Trading Commission — a sovereign allied regulator, operating under a formal Memorandum of Understanding, with statutory powers equivalent to those of the FSA — that banks under FSA supervision are manipulating a benchmark directly affecting the borrowing costs of thirty million UK citizens is not a complaint. It is intelligence. From a government. About crimes. Happening now.

The obligation to act on such intelligence is not procedural. It is not discretionary. It does not sit in a queue.

The question — who within the FSA received that intelligence, in what form, on what date, what their formal obligation was upon receiving it, what action they took, and why no mandatory escalation procedure existed for intelligence received from an allied regulator — has never been formally asked and answered in public.

This complaint asks the Commissioner to establish those facts.

## PART THREE: THE PUBLIC HARM

### 3.1 Scale of Exposure

The manipulation affected UK public bodies as well as private borrowers:

- **Housing associations**: The National Housing Federation has confirmed that approximately one third of housing association borrowing over thirty years was LIBOR-linked. Approximately £10-12 billion in LIBOR-linked housing association debt existed during the peak manipulation period (2007-2009). These institutions provide housing to approximately 2.5 million households.

- **Local authorities**: Approximately £15 billion in Lender Option Borrower Option (LOBO) loans were sold to councils between 2003 and 2008, predominantly LIBOR-benchmarked. Of these, litigation established that banks including Barclays — which was actively manipulating LIBOR throughout this period — held the option to adjust rates. The London Borough of Newham reached a multimillion pound confidential settlement with Barclays in 2022 in relation to these loans.

- **PFI contracts**: Guidance issued by HM Treasury in October 2021 acknowledges that LIBOR discontinuation "will impact the vast majority of PFI projects." The National Audit Office's 2018 review of PFI found that among the 33 largest projects reviewed, interest rate swaps were £2.3 billion out of the money — positions favourable to the banks.

- **Mortgage holders**: LIBOR-linked mortgages directly affected the rate millions of UK borrowers paid. The precise net cost to UK households from manipulation across both phases — pre-crisis upward manipulation (2005-2007) and crisis-phase lowballing (2007-2009) — has never been formally calculated. The US public sector alone estimated losses of approximately $10 billion.

### 3.2 The Outcome

Global regulatory fines totalling over $9 billion were imposed on the panel banks. Deutsche Bank was fined $2.5 billion (largest single settlement). Royal Bank of Scotland, in which UK taxpayers held an 82% stake at the time of its fine, paid $612 million.

No individual at any of these banks was imprisoned in the United Kingdom.

In January 2025, the UK Supreme Court overturned the convictions of Tom Hayes and Carlo Palombo — traders imprisoned for LIBOR manipulation. The Supreme Court found that their conduct did not constitute criminal conspiracy in the terms charged. The banks whose trading desks directed, encouraged, or tolerated that conduct were never criminally prosecuted. Individual traders bore consequences that institutions absorbed as a cost of business.

## PART FOUR: WHAT WE ARE ASKING

This complaint does not ask the Commissioner to reopen enforcement action or reverse outcomes. It asks the Commissioner to investigate and publish answers to the following specific questions:

1. **On the CFTC intelligence**: What formal communications did the FSA receive from the CFTC and other US regulatory authorities regarding LIBOR manipulation? On what dates? In what form? To which individuals or departments? What was the FSA's formal response to each such communication?

2. **On internal escalation**: What were the FSA's internal procedures — as written policy and as practised — for escalating to senior management or enforcement divisions information received from frontline supervisors about possible market manipulation by regulated firms?

3. **On the 74 communications**: The FSA's own internal audit identified 74 communications concerning LIBOR. For each of the 26 communications making direct reference to lowballing, what decision was made about escalation, by whom, and on what documented basis?

4. **On the jurisdictional gap**: When did FSA senior management become aware that LIBOR submission was not a regulated activity under FSMA 2000? What formal assessment was made of whether this gap should be closed? What advice was given to HM Treasury? Why was no action taken before the scandal became public?

5. **On equivalent obligations**: In circumstances where the FSA's own Handbook (SUP 15) required regulated firms to notify it of any matter it would reasonably expect notice of — including potential market manipulation — what equivalent obligation, if any, bound FSA supervisors who received exactly such notifications from those firms?

---

## PART FIVE: THE CASE FOR INVESTIGATION

The Commissioner has the power to investigate how the FSA exercised or failed to exercise its regulatory functions. The FSA's own internal audit confirmed that it received, and failed to act on, information about LIBOR manipulation. The TSC confirmed the FSA was two years behind its US counterpart. The PCBS found that "serious regulatory failure had contributed to the failings in banking standards."

The question this complaint pursues is not whether the FSA failed — that is established. It is whether the FSA had formal obligations, under its own rules and under any arrangements with allied regulators, that were breached when it received that intelligence and did not act.

That question goes to accountability. Not to punishment — the Commissioner cannot punish. But to the public record.

If the FSA had no formal obligation to act — if 26 direct lowballing references could land with supervisors and be met with nothing, lawfully and in compliance with the FSA's own procedures — then this complaint asks the Commissioner to say so plainly. Because that is itself the finding.

And if there were obligations that were not met, that too should be in the public record.
