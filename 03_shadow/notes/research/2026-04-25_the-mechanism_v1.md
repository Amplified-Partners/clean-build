# The Mechanism

*A documented account of how LIBOR was fixed, who knew, and what happened to them.*

---

You need a licence to own a dog. You cannot paint your house without permission. The government legislates carefully to protect us from ourselves on small things.

Fifty million people worked an extra year because it didn't apply the same care to the benchmark that priced their mortgage.

It knew. The FSA's own internal audit recorded 74 communications showing awareness of the problem. The Wheatley Review was published by HM Treasury in 2012. The knowledge is documented.

---

## What LIBOR Was

LIBOR — the London Interbank Offered Rate — was the interest rate benchmark that priced approximately $350 trillion in financial contracts at its peak. Mortgages. Business loans. Student loans. Credit cards. Derivatives. If money was lent in the English-speaking world between roughly 1985 and 2023, the price was likely set, in part, by LIBOR.

It was calculated every morning. Sixteen major banks submitted their estimate of what it would cost them to borrow from another bank. A designated administrator stripped out the highest and lowest submissions, averaged the rest, and published a single number. That number moved through the global financial system and into your mortgage payment before you'd had your coffee.

The sixteen banks on the USD panel: Bank of America, Barclays, Citigroup, Credit Suisse, Deutsche Bank, HSBC, JPMorgan Chase, Lloyds Banking Group, Rabobank, Royal Bank of Canada, Royal Bank of Scotland, Societe Generale, Norinchukin Bank, Bank of Tokyo-Mitsubishi UFJ, Portigon AG, UBS.

## The Mechanism

Here is the specific architecture that allowed this to happen.

**The BBA's Foreign Exchange and Money Markets Committee** was the governance body responsible for LIBOR. Under BBA rules, this committee had sole responsibility for "all aspects of the functioning and development of LIBOR."

The committee was chaired by a representative of one of the submitting panel banks. The same banks setting the rate also chaired and populated the committee overseeing the rate-setting process. Martin Wheatley, commissioned by HM Treasury to review LIBOR, described this as creating "a conflict of interest that precludes strong and credible governance."

When the scandal broke in 2012, a journalist asked to interview the committee chair or identify any members. The BBA declined. The committee membership — the governing body of the world's most important benchmark — was secret. The BBA said it wanted to protect members "from being lobbied." The banks setting and overseeing the world's most important interest rate benchmark couldn't be identified.

**The FSA had no jurisdiction.** Contributing to LIBOR was not a "regulated activity" under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000. The FSA was the regulator. It had no direct power over individual rate submissions. No one had sought to close this gap, and the FSA had not flagged it as a problem.

Then there is this: In March 2012, Barclays was appointed to a BBA steering committee to review LIBOR's integrity. Ninety days later, Barclays was fined $453 million for rigging LIBOR.

## What Was Known, and When

John Ewan — the BBA director responsible for LIBOR — wrote in 2005 that there was "consensus amongst banks that sterling LIBOR and US dollar are being set 3–4 basis points above the true cash rate." That is an internal memo, dated seven years before the scandal became public, from the person whose job was to run the benchmark, recording that the benchmark was wrong.

The FSA's own internal audit, commissioned and published in 2013, reviewed communications from January 2007 to May 2009. It found:

- **74 communications** in which LIBOR manipulation concerns appeared
- **26 of those contained direct references to lowballing** — deliberate underreporting of rates
- The majority were concentrated in April–June 2008

The audit's conclusion: "Taking the information cumulatively, the likelihood that lowballing was occurring **should have been considered**, and the information received should have been better managed."

The Treasury Select Committee found the FSA was **two years behind US regulators** in opening a formal LIBOR investigation. In August 2012 it concluded the FSA "failed to carry out adequate supervision," the relationship between the Bank of England and the FSA was "dysfunctional to the detriment of the public."

## The Money

Financial and related professional services contributed **£281 billion** to UK GDP in 2024 — approximately £12 in every £100 of total economic output.

Total tax contribution: **£110.2 billion** for the year ending March 2023. That is 12.3% of all UK tax receipts. More than the annual education budget. More than half the health budget.

The UK is the **world's largest net exporter of financial services** — a surplus of £92.2 billion in 2023, larger than Singapore, Switzerland, and Luxembourg combined.

The question "why didn't anyone act?" is answered partly by the numbers above. The sector that needed regulating was the sector funding the state.

## The City of London Corporation

The City of London Corporation is not a normal local authority. Several things distinguish it from every other council in the United Kingdom.

The **non-residential business vote** was abolished nationally in 1969. In the City of London it was not only preserved but expanded. Under the City of London (Ward Elections) Act 2002 — passed and on the statute book — corporations and qualifying bodies nominate workers as voters based on workforce size. As of 2009, the business vote totalled approximately **24,000**, greatly exceeding the residential vote. The banks, law firms, and financial institutions in the Square Mile have direct voting power over their own local authority. No other business in the UK has this right.

The **City Remembrancer** is a public official whose role is formally described as protecting "the City of London's interests in Parliament." The Remembrancer holds a dedicated seat behind the Speaker's Chair in the House of Commons, and a mirror-image seat in the House of Lords. The Remembrancer's department employed six lawyers at a budget of £6 million in 2011, specifically to scrutinise all prospective legislation for the Corporation's interests. The office dates to 1571. It has never been abolished. It operates today.

This is not a conspiracy theory. This is the statute book and the parliamentary record.

## The Fines

Total regulatory fines levied against LIBOR panel banks globally: over **$9 billion**.

Selected penalties:
- Barclays: $450 million (first to settle, June 2012)
- UBS: $1.5 billion
- Royal Bank of Scotland: $612 million
- Deutsche Bank: $2.5 billion (largest single settlement)
- Lloyds: £218 million
- JPMorgan Chase: $550 million criminal fine

No individual at any of these banks was imprisoned in the United Kingdom.

## Where They Went

**Derek Sach** built and ran GRG — the RBS unit that moved struggling businesses into distress, then acquired their assets through West Register. He also oversaw the period when RBS submitted false LIBOR rates. He left RBS in March 2015 and joined CVC Credit Partners, one of Europe's largest private equity firms, in an advisory role. No prosecution. No regulatory action.

**Stephen Hester** was RBS Group CEO from 2008 to 2013 — the entire active GRG and LIBOR period. In the 2024 New Year Honours, he was appointed **Knight Bachelor** for "services to Business and to the Economy." He is now Sir Stephen Hester. He chairs Nordea, one of Scandinavia's largest banks.

**Ross McEwan** succeeded Hester as RBS CEO and presided over the bank's public response to GRG. He is now Chairman of **BHP**, one of the world's largest mining companies.

**Chris Sullivan**, RBS Deputy CEO, gave incorrect evidence to the Treasury Select Committee about GRG. The RBS chairman subsequently wrote to withdraw it. Sullivan left in December 2014 and became CEO of Santander UK's Corporate and Commercial Bank.

**John Ewan**, who wrote in 2005 that LIBOR was being set wrong and did not act, faces no documented sanctions in the public record.

**Angela Knight**, BBA Chief Executive throughout the manipulation period, responded to the scandal by attributing responsibility to "other authorities and regulators." The BBA's response under her tenure was to write "regularly to the committee banks reminding them of the definition."

## What Was Broken

Not the law, primarily. The structure itself.

A benchmark priced by the people it benefited, overseen by the people it enriched, regulated by a body that had no legal jurisdiction over it, in a city whose financial institutions vote for their own council and have a permanent lobbyist with a dedicated seat in Parliament.

The $9 billion in fines was split across the same institutions. No individual went to prison in the UK. The executives moved to other large financial institutions and received honours. The mechanism was documented, named, and then resumed operating under a new name.

This is not unusual. It is, in a precise technical sense, how it is designed to work.
