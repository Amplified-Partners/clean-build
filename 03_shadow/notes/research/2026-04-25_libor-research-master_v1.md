# LIBOR — Research Master Document
*All findings from session 21 Feb 2026. Raw material — no editorial decisions made.*

---

## 1. WHAT LIBOR WAS

- London Interbank Offered Rate — the benchmark that priced approximately $350 trillion in financial contracts at its peak
- Priced mortgages, business loans, student loans, credit cards, derivatives
- Calculated daily: 16 major banks submitted their estimate of their borrowing cost. Administrator stripped highest and lowest, averaged the rest, published a single number
- Used from approximately 1985 until replaced by SONIA in 2023

**The 16 USD LIBOR panel banks:**
Bank of America, Barclays, Citigroup, Credit Suisse, Deutsche Bank, HSBC, JPMorgan Chase, Lloyds Banking Group, Rabobank, Royal Bank of Canada, Royal Bank of Scotland, Societe Generale, Norinchukin Bank, Bank of Tokyo-Mitsubishi UFJ, Portigon AG (formerly WestLB), UBS

Sources: CFTC Enforcement Order v. Barclays (June 2012); FDIC LIBOR Civil Complaint; Cohen Milstein LIBOR Antitrust Litigation

## 2. THE GOVERNANCE STRUCTURE

- The BBA's **Foreign Exchange and Money Markets (FX&MM) Committee** had "sole responsibility for all aspects of the functioning and development of LIBOR"
- The committee was **chaired by a representative of one of the submitting panel banks**
- At least one member from a panel bank for each of the 10 LIBOR currency rates
- The same banks setting the rate also chaired and populated the committee overseeing the rate-setting process
- Committee membership was secret — BBA refused to identify members publicly, saying it wanted to protect them "from being lobbied"

**Martin Wheatley (Wheatley Review, HM Treasury, September 2012):**
"The BBA acts as the lobby organisation for the same submitting banks that they nominally oversee, creating a conflict of interest that precludes strong and credible governance."

**Wheatley Review findings:**
- Systems, controls and governance arrangements were inadequate
- "A substantial overlap between the roles of contributing banks in providing inputs to compile LIBOR and in overseeing the LIBOR setting process... suggested insufficient incentive for those responsible for enforcing standards to do so with complete objectivity and independence"

**March 2012:** Barclays appointed to a BBA steering committee to review LIBOR's integrity.
**June 2012:** Barclays fined $453 million for rigging LIBOR.
Interval: 90 days.

Sources: Law Insider — FX&MM Committee definition; Harvard Law Corporate Governance Blog; Wheatley Review Final Report (HM Treasury, September 2012); Wall Street on Parade, July 2012

## 3. WHAT WAS KNOWN AND WHEN

**John Ewan** — BBA Director responsible for LIBOR, approximately 2006–2010. No previous banking experience when appointed.

In 2005, Ewan wrote internally that there was "consensus amongst banks that sterling LIBOR and US dollar are being set 3–4 basis points above the true cash rate."
This is a documented, written internal record from the person running the benchmark, dated seven years before the public scandal, recording that the benchmark was wrong. No substantive action was taken.

Sources: Risk.net / LIBOR trial reporting; MarketsWiki — John Ewan

**FSA Internal Audit Report (March 2013):**
- Reviewed communications January 2007 to May 2009
- Found **74 communications** in which LIBOR manipulation concerns appeared
- **26 contained direct references to lowballing** (deliberate underreporting)
- Majority concentrated April–June 2008
- Conclusion: "Taking the information cumulatively, the likelihood that lowballing was occurring should have been considered, and the information received should have been better managed"
- The FSA's focus on the financial crisis, combined with the fact that contributing to and administering LIBOR were not 'regulated activities', meant the FSA was "too narrowly focused in its handling of LIBOR related information"

**Why the FSA had no direct jurisdiction:**
Contributing to LIBOR was not a "regulated activity" under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000. The FSA had no direct supervisory power over individual rate submissions. This legislative gap was not sought to be closed, and not flagged publicly until after the scandal broke.

**Treasury Select Committee, "Fixing LIBOR: Some Preliminary Findings" (HC 481, August 2012):**
- FSA failed to carry out adequate supervision
- FSA was two years behind US regulators in opening a formal LIBOR investigation
- The relationship between the Bank of England and the FSA was "dysfunctional to the detriment of the public"

Sources: FSA Internal Audit Report (FCA website); FCA Press Release; TSC HC 481 (August 2012)

## 5. THE FINANCIAL SECTOR — SCALE AND TAX

- Financial and related professional services contributed **£281 billion** to UK real GVA in 2024 — approximately £12 in every £100 of total economic output
- Financial services alone: **8.6% of total GVA** in 2024
- Sector productivity: **2.7 times** whole-economy average (output per hour £104.10 vs £39.30)
- Total tax contribution: **£110.2 billion** for year ending March 2023 — **12.3% of all UK tax receipts**
- More than the annual education budget; more than half the health budget
- Banking sector alone: **£44.8 billion** for financial year to March 2024
- Financial services trade surplus: **£92.2 billion** in 2023 (note: trade surplus = exports minus imports, not profit)
- UK is the **world's largest net exporter of financial services** — larger than Singapore, Switzerland, and Luxembourg combined
- Employment: **2.5 million people** (1 in every 13 workers), two thirds outside London

Sources: TheCityUK / ONS April 2025; HMRC PAYE and Corporate Tax Receipts from the Banking Sector 2025; ONS Pink Book 2025

## 6. THE CITY OF LONDON CORPORATION

**The business vote:**
- Non-residential business vote abolished nationally in 1969
- In the City of London: preserved and expanded under the **City of London (Ward Elections) Act 2002**
- Corporations and qualifying bodies nominate workers as voters based on workforce size
- As of 2009, the business vote totalled approximately **24,000** — greatly exceeding the residential vote
- Banks, law firms, and financial institutions in the Square Mile have direct voting power over their own local authority
- No other business in the UK has this right

Source: City of London (Ward Elections) Act 2002 — legislation.gov.uk; City of London Corporation Wikipedia

## 7. THE CITY REMEMBRANCER

**The role:**
- Office dates to 1571. Never abolished. Currently operates.
- Formal function: "protecting the City of London's interests in Parliament"
- **Current Remembrancer:** Paul Wright (took office November 2023). Predecessor: Paul Double (2003–2023). Both remain on the Roll A parliamentary agents register.
- Department employs six lawyers. Annual budget: **£6 million**

**The seat:**
- Remembrancer sits in the under-gallery of the Commons chamber — visitors' seats facing the Speaker at the opposite end
- Can observe all proceedings in real time
- Cannot speak, vote, or formally intervene
- This access derives from Roll A Parliamentary Agent status (approximately 15 agents hold this)
- No other Roll A agent is simultaneously a permanent full-time statutory official of a municipality with a £6 million annual budget

**What the office actually does:**
- Monitors every Bill introduced into Parliament for anything affecting City interests
- Contacts government department officials before Bills are published (pre-legislative engagement)
- Submits written evidence to parliamentary committees
- Briefs MPs and Lords on proposed legislation
- Promotes and opposes private bills as licensed parliamentary agent
- Provides **secretariat for All-Party Parliamentary Groups** — sets agendas, drafts briefings, coordinates witnesses, manages administrative machinery of parliamentary working groups

**Transparency of Lobbying Act 2014:**
- The Remembrancer is **not registered** as a lobbyist
- Not required to be: Act covers consultant lobbyists working for third parties; in-house lobbyists (lobbying for own employer) are explicitly exempt
- No register of ministerial meetings with the Remembrancer publicly identified

**The spend:**
- Bureau of Investigative Journalism (2012): City Corporation spent **£3.9 million** specifically on "shaping legislation" in Westminster, Whitehall and Brussels in one year
- Part of a total "City representation" budget of £12.8 million annually

**Reform attempts:**
- Caroline Lucas wrote to Speaker Bercow in 2013 asking for Remembrancer to be removed from the floor of the House
- At least three parliamentary petitions filed calling for reform or abolition
- Natalie Bennett described it as "a lobbyist for elite interests holding privileged access denied to elected representatives or public interest groups"
- Corporation's consistent response: he's just a Roll A agent, performing a legitimate local authority function

Sources: City Remembrancer Wikipedia; Bureau of Investigative Journalism — "Streets paved with gold" (2012); parliament.uk FOI 2018; cityoflondon.gov.uk Parliamentary Team Update; Hansard Written Answers March 2014, October 2015

## 8. THE TRANSPARENCY OF LOBBYING ACT 2014

**Who introduced it:** Andrew Lansley. Lord Privy Seal and Leader of the House of Commons. Introduced July 2013. Steered to Royal Assent January 2014.

**The definition:**
- Act covers "consultant lobbyists" only: those paid, while VAT-registered, to lobby ministers or permanent secretaries on behalf of a third party
- In-house lobbyists are exempt by structural consequence — if you lobby for your employer, you are not lobbying "on behalf of another person"
- Trade associations claim exemption if lobbying is "no more than an incidental part" of their activities
- VAT threshold (£90,000 turnover): any lobbyist below this threshold is entirely outside the regime
- Foreign entities not registered under the UK VAT Act: also outside the regime entirely. In 2022 ORCL confirmed a Qatar-based lobbying firm did not need to declare lobbying of a UK Minister

**Scale of coverage:**
- Register currently contains **240 registrants**
- Transparency International UK: covers less than **4% of lobbyists**
- APPC estimate: covers approximately **1%** of those who engage in lobbying activity
- Industry estimated at 14,000 people and £1.9 billion at 2007 figures

**Who is not required to register (structurally exempt):**
- Every FTSE 100 in-house government affairs team (BP, Shell, HSBC, Barclays, Vodafone, BT, etc.)
- UK Finance (successor to the British Bankers' Association) — entire lobbying operation invisible
- The CBI, ABI, SMMT, and all major trade associations
- All law firms providing policy/lobbying advice
- All think tanks communicating their own analysis
- All charities' policy and lobbying teams
- The City Remembrancer
- Any foreign lobbying entity not VAT-registered in the UK

**What the Act also did (Part 2):**
- Restricted third-party campaigning by civil society organisations during election periods
- Net effect: commercial lobbying largely unregulated; civil society campaigning restricted

Sources: Transparency of Lobbying Act 2014 — legislation.gov.uk; ORCL; Commons Library SN04633; Transparency International UK; Spotlight on Corruption

## 12. NON-EXECUTIVE DIRECTORS — WHAT THEY ACTUALLY ARE

- Full board **voting rights** (common misconception that they don't vote)
- Vote on: strategy, major acquisitions, CEO pay, risk appetite, dividend policy, accounts
- Sit on audit committee, risk committee, remuneration committee
- At regulated banks: personally approved by PRA and FCA under Senior Managers and Certification Regime
- Can be fined or banned personally by regulators if they fail oversight duties
- Do not have day-to-day management authority
- Attend approximately 8–12 board meetings per year plus committee meetings
- Fee: typically £50,000–£150,000+ per year for approximately 30–40 days of work
- Senior Independent Director (Nicky Morgan's role): most senior NED position; effectively deputy chair; other NEDs raise concerns through her; she leads process to evaluate the chairman

**The Walker Review (2009, post-financial crisis):**
Explicitly criticised bank NEDs for not challenging management adequately in the run-up to the 2008 crisis. Found they were too dependent on management for information and did not challenge sufficiently.

**Qualifications required:**
- To be a Non-Executive Director at a major UK bank: PRA/FCA fitness and propriety test. No mandatory qualification. No exam. No sector experience required.
- To be a mortgage adviser and recommend a product to one person: FCA authorisation required. CeMAP qualification. Level 4 qualification. Supervised period. Ongoing CPD requirements.
