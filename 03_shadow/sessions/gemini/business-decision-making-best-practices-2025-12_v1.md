## Gemini

Making business decisions is less about "being right" every time and more about **process discipline**. The best practice is to move away from gut feeling and towards a **structured, repeatable framework** that reduces bias and clarifies trade-offs.

Here is the best-practice guide to making high-quality business decisions.

---

### 1. The Core Principle: "Frame Before You Aim"
Most bad decisions happen because the problem was ill-defined. Before looking for solutions, ensure you are solving the root cause, not just a symptom.
* **The "5 Whys" Method:** Ask "why" five times to drill down to the root cause.
* **Define Success:** What does a "good" outcome look like quantitatively? (e.g., "Increase revenue by 10%" vs. "Improve brand love").

### 2. Choose the Right Framework
Different decisions require different tools. Do not use a strategic tool for a tactical crisis.

#### **For Complex Choices: The Weighted Decision Matrix**
Use this when you have multiple options and multiple criteria (e.g., choosing a software vendor or a new office location).
1.  List your criteria (Cost, Speed, Scalability).
2.  Assign a "weight" to each (e.g., Cost = 50%, Speed = 30%).
3.  Score each option against the criteria.
4.  Multiply score by weight to get an objective winner.

#### **For Speed & Agility: The OODA Loop**
Developed by military strategists, this is best for fast-paced environments or crisis management.
* **O**bserve: Gather current data immediately.
* **O**rient: Analyze the data in context of culture and history.
* **D**ecide: Hypothesize a solution.
* **A**ct: Test the solution, then cycle back to "Observe" to see the impact.



[Image of OODA loop cycle]


#### **For Strategic Planning: SWOT Analysis**
Best for high-level direction, such as entering a new market or launching a product line.
* **Internal:** **S**trengths & **W**eaknesses (Things you control).
* **External:** **O**pportunities & **T**hreats (Market factors you don't control).

### 3. Inoculate Against Bias
Cognitive biases are the silent killers of business logic. Best practice dictates you actively fight them using these techniques:

| Bias | Definition | The Antidote |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Seeking only data that supports your preferred option. | **Devil's Advocate:** Assign one person to build a case *against* the decision. |
| **Sunk Cost Fallacy** | Continuing because "we've already spent so much." | **Zero-Based Thinking:** Ask, "If we hadn't started this yet, would we start it today?" |
| **Groupthink** | The team agrees to avoid conflict. | **Silent Voting:** Have everyone write down their vote/opinion *before* speaking. |
| **Optimism Bias** | Assuming everything will go according to plan. | **The Pre-Mortem:** Assume the project has failed 6 months from now. Ask: "What happened?" |

---

### 4. Data-Informed, Not Just Data-Driven
There is a nuanced difference that defines best practice:
* **Data-Driven:** blindly following the numbers (risky if data is incomplete).
* **Data-Informed:** using data to understand the past, but using **intuition** to navigate the future where data doesn't exist yet.

> **Rule of Thumb:** Use data for optimization (improving what exists). Use intuition for innovation (creating what doesn't exist).

### 5. The "RACI" Model for Execution
A decision is only as good as its execution. Once a choice is made, assign roles immediately using **RACI**:
* **R**esponsible: Who does the work?
* **A**ccountable: Who owns the result? (Only one person).
* **C**onsulted: Who provides input?
* **I**nformed: Who needs to be notified?

### Summary Checklist for Your Next Decision
1.  **Frame:** Have I identified the root problem?
2.  **Evidence:** Do I have 60-70% of the information? (Waiting for 100% is too slow; having <40% is guessing).
3.  **Conflict:** Have I invited dissent or a "Devil's Advocate" view?
4.  **Commitment:** Once decided, is the team "disagree and commit"?
