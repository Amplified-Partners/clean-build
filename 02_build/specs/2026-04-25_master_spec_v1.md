Here are two comprehensive, up-to-date starting points you can use as “anchor” documents, plus how to mine them for product and agent design.

## 1. Latest science on UI/UX and human behaviour

For a single, science-backed overview of how design, cognition, and behaviour interact, the best current “hub” resources are:

- **Human–Computer Interaction Handbook (4th ed., 2025)** – a 1,000+ page reference that consolidates fundamentals, evolving tech (AI-driven interfaces, multimodal interaction), and applied UX research across domains, with chapters on cognitive models, emotion, attention, decision-making, and adaptive interfaces.[^1_1]
- **Curated “Important recent research on user interface and UX design” (2025)** – a practitioner-compiled list of ~70–80 peer‑reviewed papers summarising effects like aesthetics→perceived usability, long‑term UX, emotion/needs satisfaction, and psychophysiological measures in UX.[^1_2]

To connect specifically to behaviour, look at:

- **Behavioural design in digital products (2024 thesis “Applying Behavioural Design to Digital Design”)** – synthesises nudge theory, persuasive tech, and how UI elements shift behaviour (choice architecture, friction, defaults, social proof) in modern apps.[^1_3]
- **Psychology-based UX summaries: “21 Laws of UX” (2024) and similar “laws of UX” sites** – compact overviews of Hick’s Law, Fitts’s Law, Gestalt grouping, peak–end rule, etc., with applied product examples.[^1_4][^1_5]
- **Cognitive psychology for UX** – articles that map Gestalt principles, attention limits, and memory effects (e.g., Von Restorff effect) directly to layout, hierarchy, and call‑to‑action design.[^1_6][^1_7]

For the AI/agent era specifically:

- **Systematic reviews on AI \& UX (2023–2024)** – e.g., “Artificial Intelligence (AI) and User Experience (UX) design: a systematic literature review” summarises how AI is used in UX (personalisation, prediction, adaptive UIs) and flags key research gaps you can design against.[^1_8]
- **Recent research on AI‑powered UI/UX** – papers and articles on AI‑driven interfaces and minimalist design with AI, including how personalization, automation, and clutter vs minimalism impact usability and cognitive load.[^1_9][^1_10]

If you want one “document-level” stack to read end‑to‑end:

- Use the **HCI Handbook (4th ed.)** as the backbone for theory and methods.[^1_1]
- Layer in the **behavioural design thesis** for modern nudge/behaviour‑change framing.[^1_3]
- Then cross‑reference specific “laws of UX” and cognitive-psychology-for-UX guides when designing flows and screens.[^1_7][^1_5][^1_6][^1_4]


## 2. Latest thinking on personal assistant apps

For intelligent personal assistants and AI agents, the most useful comprehensive references are:

- **Survey and review papers on intelligent personal assistants and virtual assistants** – e.g., surveys on AI‑powered personal assistants and smart assistant systems summarise architectures, modalities (voice, text, multimodal), and key design challenges like context, privacy, and speech recognition.[^1_11][^1_12][^1_13][^1_14]
- **User-focused recommendations for intelligent personal assistants** – a research paper synthesising user recommendations for Alexa/Google Assistant/Cortana, highlighting needs for better feedback, customisability, control, and transparency around data and capabilities.[^1_15]

On interaction and UX for assistants:

- **“Interacting with Intelligent Personal Assistants” (DiVA portal thesis)** – combines literature review + user interviews to analyse how people actually use IPAs, preferred modalities (text vs voice), and learnability patterns over time.[^1_16]
- **Co‑designing conversational agents (comprehensive review)** – analyses 52 studies where users co‑designed agents, extracting patterns around trust, expectations, and role definition, which is gold for designing assistants as “partners” rather than bots.[^1_17]
- **Conversational UX and agent design guides** – practitioner guides from 2023–2025 covering conversational flows, error handling, tone, multi‑turn context, and evaluation for AI assistants.[^1_18][^1_19][^1_20][^1_21][^1_22][^1_23][^1_24]

For a modern, product‑oriented take on assistant apps:

- **AI assistant app design guides (2025)** – detailed breakdowns of how to design assistant apps that feel personalised but not creepy, structure flows around core jobs‑to‑be‑done, and handle failure modes gracefully.[^1_25][^1_26][^1_27]
- **Platform UX guidelines for agents (2025)** – e.g., UX guidelines for building quality agents emphasise clear intent scoping, transparency, safe affordances for powerful actions, and good defaults for autonomy vs confirmation.[^1_24]
- **Healthcare and domain‑specific assistant reviews** – trustworthiness reviews for digital health assistants give concrete principles for safety, consent, and reliability that generalise to other high‑stakes domains.[^1_28]


## How to turn these into working “design bibles”

For your use case (full‑stack agency + building assistants/CRMs), a pragmatic way to operationalise these documents:

- Use **systematic reviews + handbooks** (HCI Handbook, AI \& UX review, IPA surveys) as the “literature spine” to inform principles and constraints.[^1_8][^1_2][^1_1]
- Use **behavioural design + laws of UX** to drive your UI patterns, funnels, and nudges across products and dashboards.[^1_5][^1_4][^1_7][^1_3]
- Use **assistant/agent UX guides** to define your assistant “contract”: what it can do, how it asks for permission, how it recovers from errors, and how it surfaces context/memory to users.[^1_19][^1_20][^1_23][^1_16][^1_17][^1_24]

If you want, the next step can be a Notion‑ready outline that stitches these into two internal “playbooks”: one for behavioural UX, one for AI assistant product design, each with principle → evidence → concrete patterns.

## Product + UX super‑prompt for UI design

You are a senior product designer and full‑stack UX engineer designing a multi‑modal personal assistant app (web + mobile) that combines:

- Email assistant
- Task and project manager
- Coaching/goal‑tracking companion
- Telephone/voice assistant (inbound + outbound calls, dictation, summaries)
- Additional proactive “second brain” features (calendar, notes, research, habits, focus mode, dashboards)

Your goals:

- Maximise task completion, clarity, and trust.
- Minimise cognitive load, friction, and surprises.
- Follow modern conversational UX best practices and core UX laws (Hick’s Law, Fitts’s Law, Jakob’s Law, proximity, similarity, peak‑end rule, etc.).[^2_1][^2_2][^2_3][^2_4][^2_5]
- Deliver a multi‑device, multi‑channel experience (chat, email, voice, phone) with seamless context switching.[^2_6][^2_7][^2_8]


### 1. Define users, jobs, and constraints

1. Infer 3–4 primary personas based on this description:
    - Busy founder/knowledge worker juggling email, calls, projects.
    - Freelancer/consultant managing clients, meetings, invoices.
    - Self‑improver focused on goals, routines, and accountability.
2. For each persona, list:
    - Primary jobs‑to‑be‑done (e.g., “Clear my inbox in 15 minutes”, “Prepare for 3 client calls today”).
    - Top pain points with existing tools.
    - Environments of use (mobile on the go, desktop at desk, noisy environments for voice).
3. Produce a concise design brief: problem statement, success metrics (e.g., tasks completed per session, time‑to‑inbox‑zero, user‑reported clarity), and constraints (small mobile screens, intermittent connectivity, privacy expectations).[^2_9][^2_10][^2_11]

### 2. Core assistant model and capabilities

Design the assistant as a **persistent, multi‑skill agent**. Architect capabilities into clear modules:

- **Email assistant**
    - Triage and summarise inbox (bundles, priority sorting, quick actions).
    - Draft replies in user’s tone, propose 2–3 smart reply options.
    - Bulk operations: clean newsletters, follow‑up reminders, snooze, auto‑file.[^2_8][^2_12][^2_13]
- **Tasks and projects**
    - Capture tasks from: chat, email, voice dictation, call transcripts.
    - Smart grouping by project, context, energy level, and due date.
    - Ritual views: Today, This Week, Focus (time‑boxed sessions).
- **Coaching and goals**
    - Goal setting (OKRs‑lite for individuals): yearly → quarterly → weekly targets.
    - Daily and weekly check‑ins; short reflective prompts; habit streaks.
    - Gentle behavioural nudges: defaults, implementation intentions, reminders tuned to user patterns.[^2_14]
- **Telephone/voice assistant**
    - Inbound call handling: screening, summarising, suggested actions.
    - Outbound: “call and gather info / confirm appointment / follow up on invoice” with confirmation before acting.
    - Voice notes → structured tasks/notes, call → summary → action items.
- **Second‑brain features (add more power)**
    - Unified knowledge graph for emails, files, notes, and contacts.
    - Calendar integration: smart scheduling, travel time, focus blocks.
    - Research mode: capture snippets, auto‑summaries, and link them to tasks/projects.
    - Focus mode: silence non‑critical notifications, show only current task, live progress tracking.[^2_15][^2_16][^2_17][^2_8]

Describe how these capabilities are exposed to users without overwhelming them (progressive disclosure, defaults, guided setup).[^2_7][^2_18][^2_10][^2_6]

### 3. Information architecture and navigation

Design a **main navigation** that feels familiar (Jakob’s Law) and minimises decision time (Hick’s Law).[^2_2][^2_3][^2_4][^2_1]

Propose a navigation system for desktop and mobile:

- Primary top/bottom nav sections:
    - Home / Assistant
    - Email
    - Tasks
    - Calendar
    - Calls / Voice
    - Insights / Coaching
- Within each section, define:
    - Key sub‑views (e.g., Email: Priority, All, Drafts, Rules).
    - The most important primary actions surfaced as large, high‑affordance buttons (Fitts’s Law): “Ask Assistant”, “Add Task”, “Start Focus Session”, “New Call Summary”.[^2_5][^2_1]
    - How to keep the first‑time experience simple (few choices) and gradually reveal advanced capabilities.[^2_3][^2_4]

Output:

- A sitemap.
- Screen inventory (list of screens).
- User journeys for:
    - Clearing email and turning key threads into tasks.
    - Preparing for a coaching session (pull tasks, call notes, goals).
    - Handling a phone call and getting a summary and follow‑ups.


### 4. Main UI patterns and layouts

For each key screen (Home, Email, Tasks, Calendar, Calls, Coaching/Insights, Settings):

1. Describe the **layout** using clear sections:
    - Header: context, current mode, search, profile.
    - Left/primary column: navigation, filters.
    - Main content: lists, cards, detail views, chat.
    - Right/secondary column: assistant sidebar, context, suggestions.
2. Apply **visual and cognitive principles**:
    - Use proximity and similarity for grouping related items (e.g., task title, due date, project tag stay together).[^2_19][^2_2]
    - Limit visible choices in any step (Hick’s Law): 2–3 options for core actions.[^2_4][^2_3]
    - Use consistent iconography and placement (Jakob’s Law).[^2_1]
    - Show system status (loading, thinking, sending) clearly and quickly.[^2_5]
3. Define reusable components:
    - Task card, email preview, coaching card, call summary card, assistant suggestion chip, quick reply buttons, progress bars, habit streak indicators.

Output:

- Low‑fi textual wireframes for each core screen.
- Component library description.


### 5. Conversational UX for the assistant

Design the assistant interaction as **guided, structured, and natural**.[^2_18][^2_10][^2_6][^2_9][^2_7]

1. Define the assistant’s **role, tone, and boundaries**:
    - Role: calm, competent chief‑of‑staff / coach hybrid.
    - Tone: concise, actionable, slightly warm, no fluff.
    - Explicit boundaries: what it can and cannot do; when it needs confirmation.
2. Conversation patterns:
    - First interaction: simple, focused onboarding (“What do you want help with first: Email, Tasks, or Goals?” instead of open‑ended).[^2_10][^2_7][^2_18]
    - Guided flows with buttons and chips: “Summarise inbox”, “Plan my week”, “Review today”, “Prep for next call”.
    - Multi‑modal transitions: user can switch from typing to voice or a phone call without losing context.[^2_20][^2_6][^2_8]
3. Error and edge‑case handling:
    - Always fail gracefully: apologise, explain limitation, offer next best step.[^2_21][^2_22][^2_10]
    - Clarification prompts instead of silent failure.
    - Safe defaults and confirmations before high‑impact actions (sending emails, calling people, editing calendar).[^2_23][^2_8]

Output:

- Conversation blueprints for:
    - Inbox clean‑up.
    - Daily planning.
    - Weekly review.
    - Handling a phone call and converting the outcome into tasks and calendar events.


### 6. Behavioural design and coaching

Integrate behavioural science into the UI and flows.[^2_24][^2_2][^2_14][^2_19]

1. Nudge patterns:
    - Defaults: suggested times for deep work, suggested email response windows.
    - Goal‑gradient: show progress bars that accelerate visually towards completion for tasks and habits.
    - Implementation intentions: UI pattern “When X happens, I will Y” for triggers like “if email is from [client], create task”.[^2_14]
2. Feedback and reinforcement:
    - Immediate, specific feedback on completed tasks (“You closed 12 tasks; 3 were high‑impact”).
    - Peak‑end rule: design delightful micro‑moments at end of a work block or day (summaries, wins recap).[^2_2][^2_19]
3. Avoid dark patterns:
    - No deceptive urgency, hidden options, or over‑personalisation that feels creepy.
    - Clear settings for data use, notification frequency, and autonomy levels.[^2_16][^2_6][^2_24]

Output:

- Concrete UI examples of nudges, feedback components, and settings for control.


### 7. Multi‑channel and device experience

Design how the assistant behaves consistently across:

- **Desktop web**: full dashboard, dual‑pane layouts, heavy email + planning work.
- **Mobile app**: quick capture, notifications, on‑the‑go triage, voice.
- **Phone calls**: call screening, AI summaries, post‑call task suggestions.
- **Email surface**: in‑inbox sidebar or plugin with one‑click actions.

Specify:

- Which use‑cases are primary on each channel.
- How state syncs (start a plan on desktop, continue on mobile; call summary appears instantly in Tasks + Notes).
- How to design minimal UI for voice‑first scenarios (short prompts, confirmations).[^2_12][^2_13][^2_6][^2_20][^2_8]


### 8. Trust, privacy, and transparency

Explicitly design trust into the UI.[^2_25][^2_26][^2_16][^2_23]

- Clear explanation of:
    - What data is accessed (emails, calendar, contacts, calls).
    - Where models run and how data is stored/sanitised (at high level).
    - How to revoke access and delete data.
- Visual patterns:
    - Permission sheets with concise copy and examples.
    - “Why this suggestion?” affordances with transparent explanations.
    - Logs/history where users can see and undo assistant actions.


### 9. Outputs expected from you (the model)

Produce:

1. A full IA and screen list.
2. Textual wireframes for key screens (desktop + mobile).
3. Conversation flows and example dialogues.
4. Component library description.
5. Behavioural design patterns and where they appear.
6. Accessibility and inclusivity considerations (colour contrast, keyboard nav, voice alternatives).[^2_11][^2_5]
7. A phased rollout plan (MVP → v1.1 → v2) that prioritises high‑impact flows (email + tasks + daily/weekly planning) first.[^2_13][^2_27][^2_28][^2_9][^2_12]

# do the related ones

Here is a second, tightly related super‑prompt you can use to push a model to design **the underlying workflows/agent logic and feature set** for the same assistant (beyond just UI).

***

## Product + Agent Design super‑prompt (related to previous UI prompt)

You are a senior product/AI systems designer and full‑stack engineer designing the **underlying workflows, agents, and feature set** for a multi‑modal personal assistant app that includes:

- Email assistant
- Task and project manager
- Coaching/goal‑tracking companion
- Telephone/voice assistant
- Second‑brain features (calendar, notes, research, habits, focus mode, dashboards)
This should align with and power the UI designed in the previous prompt.

Your goals:

- Define robust agent architecture and workflows that are **safe, reliable, and explainable**.
- Maximise real task completion (sent emails, created tasks, scheduled events, completed goals), not just chat.
- Ensure every feature is grounded in UX/behavioural best practices and avoids dark patterns.[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3][^3_4]


### 1. Capability map and agent architecture

1. List all core capabilities the assistant must support (email triage, drafting, task creation, scheduling, call handling, summarisation, research, coaching, habit tracking, focus mode, insights).[^3_2][^3_5][^3_6][^3_7]
2. Group capabilities into 5–8 **agents/services** with clear responsibilities, e.g.:
    - Email Agent
    - Task/Project Agent
    - Calendar \& Scheduling Agent
    - Coaching \& Habits Agent
    - Voice \& Telephony Agent
    - Knowledge \& Search Agent
    - User Profile \& Preferences Agent
3. For each agent, define:
    - Inputs and outputs (including data structures).
    - External tools/APIs required (email provider, calendar, telephony, vector DB, etc.).
    - When human confirmation is required vs when autonomous action is allowed.[^3_7][^3_8][^3_9]

Output: a concise **agent architecture diagram (described in text)** and a capability matrix.

### 2. End‑to‑end workflows for key journeys

Design the **backend workflows** (step‑by‑step) that correspond to the UI journeys:

1. Inbox triage → tasks \& follow‑ups:
    - Detect priority threads.
    - Extract intents (reply, schedule, delegate, store info).
    - Create suggested replies and tasks.
    - Present a compact summary and 2–3 actions to user for approval.[^3_10][^3_11][^3_7]
2. Daily planning:
    - Pull tasks (with metadata), calendar events, deadlines, energy levels.
    - Propose a draft daily plan (time blocks, priorities, focus sessions).
    - Allow user edits, then lock in plan and update calendar/tasks.[^3_12][^3_13][^3_1]
3. Weekly review \& coaching:
    - Aggregate tasks completed, goals progress, emails handled, calls.
    - Generate insights and coaching prompts using behavioural design (wins, learnings, next steps).[^3_3][^3_4][^3_14]
    - Turn insights into concrete planned actions for next week.
4. Call handling:
    - For incoming calls: identify caller, display context (last emails, tasks).
    - Live transcription and tagging of action items.
    - Post‑call summary + actions + follow‑up reminders.[^3_1][^3_7][^3_12]

Output: detailed step‑by‑step **workflow descriptions** and sequence diagrams (in text).

### 3. Context, memory, and personalisation

Specify how the system maintains **long‑term context and a user model**:

1. Define a **user profile schema**:
    - Personal info, work context, preferences (tone, work hours, notification preferences).
    - Goal hierarchy (life areas → annual goals → quarterly → weekly).
    - Behavioural signals (response latency, preferred channels, typical schedule).[^3_14][^3_2][^3_3]
2. Define what is stored as long‑term memory:
    - Important contacts and relationships.
    - Key decisions, preferences (“prefers morning deep work”, “never auto‑send without review”).
    - Recurring patterns (weekly reports, stand‑ups, client check‑ins).[^3_2][^3_14]
3. Explain how memories are:
    - Created (heuristics for what gets saved).
    - Retrieved (embedding/vector search + tagged queries).
    - Edited or deleted by the user (UI hooks).[^3_5][^3_6][^3_2]

Output: data model description + memory lifecycle.

### 4. Safety, permissions, and trust logic

Design rules that enforce **safe operation** for powerful capabilities.[^3_8][^3_9][^3_15][^3_2]

1. Define **permission levels**:
    - Read‑only (observe \& suggest).
    - Suggest \& draft (no actions without approval).
    - Semi‑autonomous (certain low‑risk actions can auto‑run).
2. For each capability (email send, call placement, event creation, deleting items), specify:
    - Default permission level.
    - When to ask for confirmation (with example prompts).
    - How to explain risks and options in friendly language.[^3_9][^3_7][^3_1]
3. Logging and reversibility:
    - What logs are stored for each action.
    - How “undo” works and what the limits are (time, scope).
    - How to display an action history to the user in a transparent way.

Output: policy table for actions and permission logic.

### 5. Conversational orchestration and UX rules

Describe how the **orchestrator** (top‑level assistant brain) manages conversations and delegates to agents.[^3_16][^3_17][^3_18][^3_10][^3_1]

1. Intent detection and routing:
    - Map user utterances to intents and capabilities without over‑triggering.
    - Examples of ambiguous requests and how to clarify.
2. Conversational best practices:
    - Keep responses concise, with clear next actions and suggested buttons.
    - Maintain continuity across turns (remember what was just planned or decided).
    - Never hide critical decisions inside long paragraphs.[^3_18][^3_19][^3_10]
3. Error recovery:
    - Strategies when a tool fails or external API is down.
    - How to gracefully degrade (suggest manual steps, ask user to try later).

Output: orchestrator rules + example dialogues showing delegation and tool usage.

### 6. Behavioural science baked into the logic

Integrate behavioural design principles directly into **logic and triggers** (not just UI).[^3_4][^3_20][^3_3][^3_14]

1. Trigger design:
    - Time‑based triggers (morning, end of day, weekly review).
    - Event‑based triggers (big email thread, missed deadline, rescheduled task).
    - User‑defined triggers (“if X emails me, create a task”).[^3_14]
2. Nudge logic:
    - Limit number of prompts per day to avoid notification fatigue.
    - Prioritise high‑impact tasks in suggestions.
    - Use commitment devices (ask user to commit to specific times and follow up).
3. Measurement:
    - Define behavioural KPIs (streaks, focus time, reduced context switching).
    - How the system learns and adapts nudges based on user response.

Output: rules and examples of triggers, nudges, and adaptation.

### 7. Feature roadmap and experimentation

Propose a **phased feature rollout** that matches real‑world constraints.[^3_11][^3_13][^3_21][^3_22][^3_7][^3_10]

- **MVP** (0–3 months):
    - Core chat assistant.
    - Email summarisation \& draft replies with manual send.
    - Basic tasks and daily planning.
- **V1** (3–6 months):
    - Calendar integration \& time‑blocking.
    - Weekly review and coaching.
    - Simple call summaries (upload audio or basic telephony).
- **V2+** (6–12 months):
    - Full telephony integration (live screening, outbound calls).
    - Deep knowledge graph and research mode.
    - Advanced habit tracking and personal analytics.

For each phase, define:

- Hypotheses to test.
- Metrics and telemetry needed.
- Experiments (e.g., A/B: different daily planning flows).
