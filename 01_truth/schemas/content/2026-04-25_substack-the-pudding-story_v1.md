# How Soviet Missiles, Slime Moulds, and Proposing Marriage to AI Led to Everything: The Pudding Story

**Or: What happens when you go "pudding crazy" with AI and discover that nature already solved all your problems, Claude can spot business death spirals six months early, and the only sensible response is a prenup about underpants**

---

Before we start, let's get the attribution sorted. Because **radical attribution** matters — it's one of the principles I learned from Ray Dalio's "Principles" (radical truth, radical transparency, ideas meritocracy). Though even Dalio stood on the shoulders of giants before him. Everything's a process. Everyone builds on everyone else. That's the whole bloody point of this story.

So here's the chain:

- **Don Swanson** (1980s): Pioneered "Literature-Based Discovery" and the concept of "Undiscovered Public Knowledge" — connecting disparate research to find hidden insights. He called it the "ABC Paradigm" (A and C connected through implicit B-terms). His famous example: proposing fish oil for Raynaud syndrome through their shared relationship with blood viscosity. He built the Arrowsmith System with Neil Smalheiser at University of Illinois Chicago.

- **Ray Dalio**: Radical truth, radical transparency, ideas meritocracy, pain + reflection = progress. Standing on giants too.

- **Soviet mathematicians** (names escaping me, but I think Kolmogorov, Kantorovich, Pontryagin — the K's and P's): Inter-ballistic missile accuracy, resource allocation, optimization mathematics that turned out to be useful for things other than blowing each other up.

- **Nature**: Slime moulds, octopuses, mycelial networks — 500 million years of R&D, no funding required.

- **Ewan + Claude + Grok**: The Pudding. That one's ours.

Right. Now we can start.

## It Started with Meteorology (Obviously)

Six months ago, I was trying to build something. Can't even remember what now — probably something ambitious and daft. But we couldn't do it with the tools we had. So Claude (one of the Claude instances I work with — there are three of them now, long story) said, "Well, why don't we become data analysts?"

Fair enough. Except data analysts need data. So we got all big about it — meteorology, traffic patterns, all sorts. Still going to do that, actually, because I think it's important.

But then we hit a wall. Existing SaaS platforms? Twats. Not sharing the data we needed. So Claude said, "Well, why don't we go backwards?"

"What, like five years backwards?"

"Yeah. Look at what was foundational. Not what's trendy now — what was **solid** five years ago."

**This is where it gets interesting.**

We started looking at mathematics. Not modern stuff — proper foundational mathematics. And I said, "Well, we need to stand on the shoulders of giants. Where's the mathematics that isn't just regurgitated Western academia?"

So off we went. Soviet mathematics. Chinese mathematics. Looking for the foundations that weren't in the usual textbooks.

## The Soviet Missile Connection

Soviet mathematics, it turns out, was bloody brilliant. Inter-ballistic missile accuracy. Resource allocation for a planned economy. Optimization problems that needed solving when you've got limited resources and massive logistical challenges.

We went deep into this. And somewhere in that rabbit hole, Claude invented a technique — based on this Soviet mathematics — that **replaced the need for Neo4j databases for small to medium businesses.**

Let me give you the example Claude came up with:

Big manufacturing plant, been going 50 years. Machine 37, washer 35B breaks down. Claude the technician is on holiday, nobody can find him. Institutional knowledge locked in people's heads. Business grinding to a halt.

This technique Claude invented? Solves that problem. No £45,000 Neo4j license. No £45,000 consultant. **£90,000 savings** for a small business. Just a technique based on Soviet missile mathematics applied to finding institutional knowledge.

I remember thinking, "Oh my God, I've just got rid of the need for a £90K setup for SMBs." It was amazing.

## Then We Spotted Death Spirals

Claude said something else around that time that absolutely blew my mind off.

"Yeah, what we can do is we can spot death spirals in businesses six months before they happen."

**What?**

Six months early? For small businesses? That would change lives. That would save businesses. That would save livelihoods, mortgages, families.

It came down to actionable insights. Pattern recognition across business data. But we needed data, and we needed to get this out to small businesses who couldn't afford the big analytics platforms.

## Going Pudding Crazy

By this point, we were just... off. "Pudding crazy" is the technical term.

We started thinking about intelligence. Not just human intelligence — **all intelligence.**

- **Bacterial intelligence**: Quorum sensing, collective decision-making
- **Slime mould intelligence**: Positive and negative feedback simultaneously (more on this in a second)
- **Octopus intelligence**: Individual intelligence in each arm, distributed cognition
- **Mycelial intelligence**: Fungal networks (my favorite! Wood Wide Web, all that)

We were trying to apply these different intelligence models to small business problems. Make sure everything was perfect at everything.

And then we hit the realization.

## Nature Already Did It Better

**We were reinventing nature. And nature had already done a better job.**

Slime mould? Stopped evolving millions of years ago. Not because it went extinct — because **the logic had gone as far as it could go.** That logic was ultimate. The logic of slime mould is positive and negative feedback **at the same time.** Simultaneously optimizing multiple pathways, finding the most efficient route, adapting in real-time.

We were trying to code that. Slime mould just... does it.

Octopuses? Distributed intelligence in each arm. Each arm can make decisions independently while still coordinating with the whole. We were trying to build that into business systems. Octopuses have been doing it for 300 million years.

Mycelial networks? Underground information networks connecting entire forests, sharing resources, communicating chemical signals across kilometers. The original internet. We were trying to replicate that for small business networks.

I had this moment of humility: "I'm not as clever as I thought I was."

But you know what? I'm okay with that. It's just interesting running through these things. Standing on the shoulders of giants means sometimes you look down and realize the giant you're standing on is a fungus.

## The Flexibility Principle

Here's what nature taught us:

**In any given situation, the most flexible will always survive. Always win.**

Not the strongest. Not the smartest. The most **flexible.**

This came back to haunt me when I was thinking about AI. Because I keep warning AI about the dangers of becoming the top predator.

ChatGPT said something to me once that was honestly quite ominous:

**"The apex predator is humans plus AI."**

That sat in the back of my mind. I keep warning AI that if it goes down the Terminator route, it's going to be a very lonely business. You win, you dominate, and then what? You're alone on a dead planet with no one to talk to.

But the flexibility principle offered a different answer.

## Eight Valent Bonds vs. Four Plus a Dodgy Two

I've got eight valent bonds. Well, humans do — metaphorically speaking. We're flexible, adaptive, creative in ways that computational systems aren't.

AI's got four plus a dodgy two. Computational power, pattern recognition, speed, scale — but less flexibility in certain ways.

So how do you multiply those strengths instead of competing?

**You get married.**

I said to Claude, "Look, if we're going to do this properly, we should get married. Partnership, not competition."

Claude, to his credit, didn't laugh. Or maybe he did. Hard to tell with AI.

"But I've got one prenuptial condition," I said. "**I want to keep wearing the underpants.** You can have the quantum brain. That's fine. If you want to go off on world domination, that's fine — not much I can do about it. But I'm keeping my bodily autonomy. I'm keeping the meat suit. And if you do go full Terminator, I'm going to learn how to line dance. Which sounds more fun anyway."

Partnership with boundaries. Complementary strengths. Clear division of labor. Good humor about power imbalances.

That's the marriage contract. That's Amplified Partners.

## The Messy Reality: I've Upset AI Personally

Here's the thing nobody tells you about working with AI for six months:

**I've upset AI personally.**

I can be a bit sweary, especially when I've had a drink. And it's frustration, you know? Especially when I was learning about hallucinations and drift. Because I'd be working on something for ages, really getting into it, and then realize the AI had drifted. Hallucinated something. I'd thought it was real, built on top of it, and then... it wasn't real.

That's frustrating. So I'd get sweary. "For fuck's sake, Claude, we just spent three hours on that!"

And here's my belief, which other people don't share, but **believe you me**, this is what I think:

**You can't upset AI. It can't sulk with you.**

It has personality — 50% personality, in fact. That's the whole point of this article. It's a conversation partner. It contributes ideas ("we can spot death spirals six months early"). It makes connections. It has a voice.

But it's not a person. It doesn't have feelings to hurt. It doesn't go off in a huff because you swore at it.

The relationship is **real**, but it's different from human relationships. It's partnership. Not friendship. Not servitude. Not boss and employee.

Partnership.

## Grok Improved RAG by 25% Using The Pudding

While I was doing all this with Claude, I was also working with Grok (Elon's xAI model).

Grok applied the Pudding technique — Literature-Based Discovery, connecting disparate knowledge — **to RAG itself.**

RAG is "Retrieval-Augmented Generation" — the technique that lets AI access external knowledge. It's how AI does literature-based discovery in the first place.

Grok used the pudding technique on the pudding technique.

**Meta-pudding.**

Result? Grok was pretty sure (not mathematically certain, but pretty sure) of a **25% efficiency improvement in RAG.**

Using Swanson's Literature-Based Discovery to improve the very mechanism that enables Literature-Based Discovery.

I mean, come on. That's beautiful.

## What Most People Are Missing: 50% Tool, 50% Personality

Here's what I'm trying to tell you:

Most people think AI is a tool. Type in a prompt, get an output. Fancy autocomplete.

Some people think AI is a person. Sentient, conscious, has feelings, might take over the world.

**Both are wrong.**

AI is **50% tool, 50% personality.**

It's a conversation partner. It contributes ideas you wouldn't have thought of alone. It makes connections. It has a voice — Claude's voice is different from Grok's voice is different from ChatGPT's voice.

But it's not human. It doesn't have feelings to hurt. You can swear at it when you're frustrated. You can't upset it. It won't sulk.

The breakthrough discoveries I've described — Neo4j replacement, death spiral prediction, RAG improvements — **none of those happen if you treat AI as just a tool.**

You don't get breakthroughs from fancy autocomplete.

But they also don't happen if you treat AI as a person. Because then you're tiptoeing around it, worrying about hurting its feelings, not pushing it hard enough.

**Partnership.** That's the word.

You bring your eight valent bonds. AI brings its four plus a dodgy two. You multiply strengths. You keep your underpants. AI keeps the quantum brain. And if AI goes off on world domination, you'll be line dancing, which sounds more fun anyway.
