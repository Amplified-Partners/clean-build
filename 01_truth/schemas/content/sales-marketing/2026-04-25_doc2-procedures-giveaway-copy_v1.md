**2. PROCESS IMPROVEMENT METHODOLOGY**

The Amplified Process Maturity Score (AMPS) is a structured way to take any business process apart, measure how good it is, improve it, and put it back together. It works for any process in any business --- from how you answer the phone to how you handle invoicing.

**The 7-Step Cycle**

Every process improvement follows these seven steps, in order:

-   **Decompose:** Break the process into its smallest individual steps. Do not assume you know them --- watch, record, and list every single action.

-   **Score:** Rate each step on a 0--10 scale across six weighted dimensions (see below). Be honest. A generous score helps nobody.

-   **Research:** For any step scoring below 7, research how others solve the same problem. Look outside your industry --- plumbers can learn from dentists, and dentists can learn from retail.

-   **Design:** Create an improved version of the weak steps. Write it down. If it is not written down, it is not a process.

-   **Test:** Run the new version alongside the old one. Measure both. Do not guess which is better --- prove it.

-   **Validate:** Confirm the improvement is real, repeatable, and sustainable. One good day is not validation. Two weeks of consistent improvement is.

-   **Reassemble:** Put the improved steps back into the full process. Document everything. Train everyone who touches it.

**The 0--10 Scoring Scale**

Each process step is scored across six weighted dimensions. This is not a gut-feel exercise --- each dimension has a specific definition:

  --------------- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- ------------
  **Dimension**   **What It Measures**                                                          **Weight**
  Efficiency      How quickly does this step complete without wasting time or resources?        High
  Accuracy        How often does this step produce the correct result first time?               High
  Reliability     Does this step work consistently, every time, regardless of who does it?      High
  Scalability     Can this step handle more volume without breaking?                            Medium
  Clarity         Can a new person understand and follow this step without extra explanation?   Medium
  Cost            What does this step cost in time, money, or effort relative to its value?     Medium
  --------------- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- ------------

Score each dimension from 0 (completely broken) to 10 (cannot meaningfully improve). The weighted average across all six dimensions gives you the step\'s AMPS score.

-   **Ship threshold:** A process step needs a score of 7.0 or above before you consider it \"good enough\" to use.

-   **Gold standard:** A score of 9.0 or above means the step is excellent and should be documented as a reference example.

**Worked Example: A Plumbing Business**

Scenario: A small plumbing company wants to improve how they handle emergency call-outs.

**Step 1: Decompose**

Break it down: Customer calls → receptionist answers → captures details → checks diary → assigns plumber → plumber confirms → travel → arrive → diagnose → fix → invoice → follow-up.

That is 12 steps. Most business owners think it is 3 or 4.

**Step 2: Score**

Rate each of those 12 steps. You might find that \'captures details\' scores only 4/10 on accuracy because the receptionist does not have a standard checklist, and half the time the plumber arrives without knowing the boiler brand.

**Step 3: Research**

How do veterinary emergency clinics capture call details? They use triage scripts. Could a similar script work for plumbing emergencies? Almost certainly yes.

**Steps 4--7: Design, Test, Validate, Reassemble**

Create a 6-question emergency checklist. Test it for two weeks. If accuracy improves from 4/10 to 8/10, validate that it holds, then make it permanent.

**3. BUILD QUALITY FRAMEWORK**

The Build Quality Framework ensures that when you change a process, you do not accidentally make things worse. It is a safety net with teeth. This is the simplified version --- designed for any business to use without specialist knowledge.

**The 6-Stage Pipeline**

Every process change moves through six stages before it goes live:

  ----------------- -------------------------------------------- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
  **Stage**         **What Happens**                             **Plain English**
  1\. Decompose     Break process into atomic steps              Take it apart so you can see every piece
  2\. Score 0--10   Rate each step on the AMPS scale             Be brutally honest about how good each piece actually is
  3\. Research      Find better approaches for weak steps        Look at how other people --- especially in other industries --- solve this
  4\. Test          Run the improved version alongside the old   Try the new way, but do not throw away the old way yet
  5\. Reassemble    Rebuild the full process with improvements   Put the better pieces back into the whole
  6\. Attribute     Document where every improvement came from   Give credit. Say what works, what came from where, and why
  ----------------- -------------------------------------------- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Process Readiness Score (PRS)**

The PRS is a single number that tells you whether a process is ready to use. It is calculated from the AMPS scores of all individual steps.

-   **Below 7.0:** Do not ship. The process is not ready. Improve the weakest steps first.

-   **7.0 to 8.9:** Ship threshold. The process is safe to use and will deliver reliable results.

-   **9.0 and above:** Gold standard. This process is excellent. Document it as a reference for others.

**The Ship Threshold Principle**

This is the single most important rule: if a process scores below 7.0, do not use it in your business. Full stop.

Why 7.0? Because below that number, the process has too many failure points. You will spend more time fixing problems than doing work. A 6 feels close to good enough --- it is not. The gap between 6 and 7 is where most business problems live.

**Quality Gates for Your Business**

A quality gate is a checkpoint that must be passed before moving to the next stage. Think of it like a plumbing inspection --- you do not plasterboard over pipes until someone has checked they do not leak.

-   Gate 1: Is the process written down? (If no, write it down first.)

-   Gate 2: Has every step been scored? (If no, score it before changing anything.)

-   Gate 3: Has the improvement been tested for at least two weeks?

-   Gate 4: Is the result measurably better than what it replaced?

-   Gate 5: Is the improvement documented and attributed?

**4. THE PUDDING TECHNIQUE**

PUDDING stands for Pattern-based Universal Description and Discovery through Integrated Notation for Generative synthesis. It sounds complicated. The idea behind it is simple: find useful connections between things that nobody thought to connect.

**Origins: Don Swanson\'s Literature-Based Discovery**

In 1986, a librarian named Don Swanson at the University of Chicago made a remarkable discovery. He found that fish oil (discussed in cardiology journals) and Raynaud\'s disease (discussed in rheumatology journals) were connected --- but nobody knew, because the two sets of researchers never read each other\'s papers.

Swanson called this \"undiscovered public knowledge.\" The information was already out there. It just had not been connected.

PUDDING applies the same principle to business knowledge.

**The PUDDING Principle: Prospecting, Not Expecting**

When you go looking for cross-domain insights, you are prospecting. You are not expecting a specific answer --- you are looking for patterns that might be valuable. Most will not be. Some will be worth a fortune.

The key insight is the difference between matching and mashup:

-   **Matching finds similarity:** 5 + 5 = 10. You find things that confirm what you already know. Useful, but not transformative.

-   **Mashup finds emergence:** 5 + 5 = 13. You combine ideas from different fields and create something that did not exist before. This is where breakthroughs happen.

**Simple Labelling for Business Knowledge**

The full PUDDING system uses a 4-part label: WHAT.HOW.SCALE.TIME. For a small business, you can simplify this to three questions about any piece of business knowledge:

-   **What kind of thing is it?** (A procedure, a principle, a fact, an opinion)

-   **What area does it serve?** (Marketing, operations, cashflow, staffing, customer service)

-   **How long will it stay relevant?** (Timeless principle vs. dated tactic)

Label your knowledge this way and you can search it later. More importantly, you can spot when two different areas share a pattern that might create value.

**How Any Business Owner Can Find Cross-Domain Insights**

You do not need a database or AI for this. You need curiosity and a simple habit:

1.  Read outside your industry. If you are a plumber, read about dentistry, retail, logistics --- anything that is not plumbing.

2.  When you see something clever, write it down with a label: what it is, what field it came from, and what problem it solves.

3.  Every month, review your notes. Look for two ideas from different fields that share an underlying pattern.

4.  Ask: \'Could this combination solve a problem in my business that neither idea solves alone?\'

**Scoring Discoveries**

Not every cross-domain connection is valuable. Use this rubric to separate gold from noise:

  ------------------- ----------------------------------------------------------------- ------------
  **Factor**          **What To Ask**                                                   **Weight**
  Domain Distance     How far apart are the source fields? (Closer = less novel)        High
  Pattern Alignment   Do the underlying mechanics actually match?                       Very High
  Gap Filling         Does this solve a known problem in your business?                 High
  Tension Bonus       Does the combination feel surprising or counterintuitive?         Medium
  SMB Applicability   Can a small business actually use this without huge investment?   High
  Novelty             Has someone already made this connection?                         Low
  ------------------- ----------------------------------------------------------------- ------------

A discovery needs to score well on Pattern Alignment and Gap Filling to be worth pursuing. High Domain Distance without Pattern Alignment is just randomness.

**5. THE RAPID INTELLIGENCE CYCLE**

The Rapid Intelligence Cycle (RIC) is a system for regularly reviewing your business data, spotting patterns, and making improvements at the right speed. Not everything needs the same urgency. Some things need checking every night. Some only need looking at once a quarter.

**4 Velocity Tiers**

  ---------- --------------- --------------------------------------------------- ----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **Tier**   **Frequency**   **What Goes Here**                                  **Example**
  Nightly    Every day       Critical operations, urgent flags                   Did all emergency jobs get completed? Any complaints today?
  Weekly     Every week      Performance patterns, short-term trends             Are we quoting fast enough? How many leads went cold?
  Monthly    Every month     Financial health, process scores, PUDDING reviews   Are our AMPS scores improving? Any cross-domain insights this month?
  Glacial    Quarterly+      Strategic direction, market position, big bets      Should we enter a new market? Is our pricing model still right?
  ---------- --------------- --------------------------------------------------- ----------------------------------------------------------------------

**Setting Up a Basic Intelligence Cycle**

You do not need software for this. You need discipline and 15 minutes a day.

**Nightly (5 minutes)**

-   Review: What went well today? What went wrong?

-   Flag anything that needs immediate attention tomorrow.

-   One sentence on what you learned.

**Weekly (30 minutes, same day each week)**

-   Review your nightly notes from the past 7 days.

-   Spot any patterns --- same problem three times? That is a process issue, not bad luck.

-   Pick one process to investigate using the AMPS cycle from Section 2.

**Monthly (1 hour)**

-   Review financial performance against your targets.

-   Score your key processes using the AMPS framework.

-   Do one PUDDING review --- read something outside your industry and look for useful connections.

**Glacial (Half day, once per quarter)**

-   Step back from the business and think about direction, not operations.

-   Review what your competitors are doing.

-   Ask: \'Are we solving the right problems, or just getting faster at solving the wrong ones?\'

**PUDDING Discovery vs Kaizen Improvement**

These are two different tools for two different situations:

-   **PUDDING:** Use when you need a breakthrough. When the current approach has been optimised and you are stuck. When you need fresh thinking from outside your field.

-   **Kaizen:** Use when you have a working process that needs to get incrementally better. Small, continuous improvements. The Japanese manufacturing philosophy of \'change for better.\'

The transition works like this: PUDDING gives you a new idea. You test it. If it works, it enters your Kaizen cycle for continuous refinement.

**The 2-Week Stability Rule**

Never make a change permanent until it has been stable for at least two weeks. This is not arbitrary --- it is based on the minimum time needed to distinguish a real improvement from a lucky streak.

-   Week 1: The change is new. Everyone is paying attention. Performance often looks artificially good (the Hawthorne Effect).

-   Week 2: Attention fades. Real performance emerges. If the improvement holds through week 2, it is likely genuine.

-   After 2 weeks: Make it permanent, document it, and train everyone on the new approach.

**6. VOICE-FIRST INTERFACE DESIGN PRINCIPLES**

Most business tools are designed for people who sit at desks. Tradespeople, small business owners, and people who work with their hands do not sit at desks. Voice-first design solves this.

**Calm Technology Principles**

Calm Technology is an approach to design that respects the user\'s attention. It was first described by Mark Weiser and John Seely Brown at Xerox PARC in 1995. The core idea: technology should inform without demanding attention.

For business tools, this means:

-   Information should move from the periphery to the centre of attention only when relevant.

-   The tool should make the user feel calm, not anxious.

-   Technology is best when it does not feel like technology.

**The Three-Task Maximum Rule**

A busy person can hold three things in their head at once. Not five. Not seven. Three.

Every interface, every screen, every voice interaction should present a maximum of three tasks or options. If you have more than three, prioritise. The rest can wait.

This is supported by cognitive load research from George Miller (1956) --- while his famous paper suggested 7±2 items for short-term memory, subsequent research by Nelson Cowan (2001) revised this to 3--4 items for practical working memory. In a noisy, stressful work environment, three is the safe number.

**\"Like a Considerate Senior Nurse\"**

This is the tone guidance for any business tool. When the system talks to a user --- whether by voice, text, or notification --- it should sound like a considerate senior nurse:

-   Calm, not panicked. Even when delivering bad news.

-   Direct, not vague. Say what needs to happen.

-   Supportive, not judgemental. The user is trying their best.

-   Short. A nurse does not give a lecture. They give clear instructions.

-   Competent. The user trusts this voice because it clearly knows what it is talking about.

**Why Voice-First Reduces Friction**

A plumber under a sink cannot type. A hairdresser with wet hands cannot swipe. A van driver should not be looking at a screen.

Voice input removes the biggest barrier to using business tools: the requirement to stop working. With voice-first design, the tool fits around the work, not the other way around.

**The Pre-Translator Pattern**

Voice input is only useful if it is accurate. The pre-translator pattern solves this:

1.  The system captures raw voice input.

2.  A translation layer cleans up accent, dialect, and speech patterns before any processing happens.

3.  If the system is not confident about what was said, it asks the user to repeat --- it never guesses.

4.  The system learns the user\'s voice over time (approximately 72 hours of on-device training).

This means a Geordie accent, a Scottish accent, or any regional dialect gets properly understood --- not flattened into received pronunciation and misinterpreted.

**7. DASHBOARD DESIGN THAT WORKS**

Most business dashboards are designed by people who like data. The people who need dashboards usually do not like data --- they like knowing their business is healthy and that they will make it home for their kids\' bedtime. Design for them, not for data people.

**Frame Data Around Personal Life Goals**

The Reticular Activating System (RAS) is the part of your brain that decides what to pay attention to. It prioritises things connected to your personal goals and interests. This is why you notice a car everywhere after you decide to buy one.

Use this in dashboard design: frame business data in terms of what the person actually cares about. Not \'process efficiency increased 12%\' but \'you saved 3 hours this week --- that is a football match with your kids.\'

When a number connects to something personal, the brain pays attention. When it does not, the brain ignores it. This is not manipulation --- it is presenting the same information in a way the brain can actually use.

**\"Time Saved\" as Universal Currency**

Every business owner understands time. Not all of them understand margins, conversion rates, or process efficiency scores. But everyone understands \'you got back 4 hours this week.\'

Wherever possible, translate business metrics into time. Time is the one resource no one can make more of, and the one every small business owner wishes they had.

**14-Year-Old Reading Level**

If a 14-year-old cannot understand your dashboard at a glance, it is too complicated. This is not about intelligence --- it is about cognitive load. A busy person scanning a screen while their phone rings has the effective attention of a teenager scrolling social media. Design for that reality.

Rules:

-   Short sentences. No subclauses.

-   Common words. \'Profit\' not \'margin optimisation.\'

-   Numbers with context. \'£2,400 up from £1,800\' not \'33% MoM increase.\'

**Zero Corporate Jargon**

If a word would not be used in a pub conversation, it does not belong on a dashboard. Replace:

  ----------------------- --------------------------
  **Instead of\...**      **Say\...**
  KPI                     The number that matters
  Conversion rate         How many people said yes
  Revenue pipeline        Money coming in soon
  Churn rate              Customers we lost
  Stakeholder alignment   Everyone agrees
  Bandwidth               Time available
  ----------------------- --------------------------

**What to Show vs What to Hide**

Show the three numbers that matter most today. Hide everything else behind a \'more details\' option. If someone needs to see everything, they can tap through. But the default view should answer one question: \'Is my business healthy right now?\'

-   **Always show:** Cash position, biggest task today, time saved this week.

-   **Show on request:** Trend lines, comparisons, process scores.

-   **Hide completely:** Raw data, technical metrics, anything that requires explanation.

**8. CLIENT ONBOARDING WITHOUT FRICTION**

The worst way to onboard a new client is to hand them a form. They are busy. They do not know where half their data is. And they are already nervous about whether this is going to be worth their time.

Better approach: extract what you need from what they already have.

**The DocBench Approach**

DocBench is a data extraction engine that works from existing IT --- emails, invoices, contracts, support tickets, spreadsheets, and anything else a business already generates. Instead of asking the client to fill in forms, DocBench reads what already exists and structures it.

The principle behind this is simple: every business already documents itself. It just does not know it. Every email sent, every invoice raised, every contract signed --- it is all data. DocBench finds it, cleans it, and structures it.

**Four Output Formats**

Everything extracted goes into one of four formats:

  --------------------- ------------------------------------------------------------------ ----------------------------------------------------------
  **Format**            **What It Contains**                                               **Who Uses It**
  Raw Text              Unstructured content exactly as found                              Reference archive --- the original source
  Structured JSON       Clean, machine-readable data                                       Systems that need to process or display the information
  Process / Logic       Step-by-step procedures extracted from how the business operates   Process improvement, training, documentation
  Principles / Values   The underlying rules and beliefs that drive decisions              Culture documentation, AI training, onboarding new staff
  --------------------- ------------------------------------------------------------------ ----------------------------------------------------------

**The Business Vault**

Once extracted, all business information goes into a single, secure repository: the business vault. Think of it as a brain for your business --- one place where everything lives, properly organised and searchable.

Key principles:

-   The data belongs to the business, always. It is never shared, sold, or used for anything the business has not agreed to.

-   Single source of truth. No duplicate documents, no conflicting versions.

-   Searchable. Find anything in seconds, not hours.

-   Secure. Encrypted, access-controlled, and backed up.

For a small business, the simplified version of a vault is a well-organised shared folder with a naming convention and a simple index document. The principle --- one truth, well organised --- matters more than the technology.

**9. TESTING YOUR AI**

If you are using AI in your business --- chatbots, automated emails, data analysis, anything --- you need to test it before you trust it. Not once. Regularly. AI systems can be confidently wrong, and confident wrong answers destroy trust faster than no answer at all.

**The Universal Quality Gate**

Before deploying any AI feature, ask two questions:

**1. Does it help the person?** If the answer is no, do not ship it. It does not matter how clever it is.

**2. Could we do better?** If the answer is yes, iterate before shipping. Good enough is not good enough when it is someone\'s livelihood.

These two questions are deceptively simple. They eliminate vanity features (clever but not helpful) and premature deployment (helpful but not ready).

**Basic Chaos Scenarios to Test Before Trusting AI**

Chaos testing means deliberately trying to break things to find out where they fail. Here are scenarios any business can test:

  ---------------------------- -------------------------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------------------------
  **Scenario**                 **What You Are Testing**                                 **How to Test It**
  Gibberish input              Does the AI handle nonsense gracefully?                  Type random characters and see what happens
  Contradictory instructions   Does the AI notice when you ask for impossible things?   Ask it to do two opposite things at once
  Missing information          Does the AI ask for what it needs, or guess?             Give it half the information and see if it asks for the rest
  Edge cases                   Does the AI handle unusual situations?                   Give it your weirdest real customer scenario
  Wrong information            Does the AI catch obvious errors?                        Feed it incorrect data and see if it flags it
  Volume stress                Does the AI cope under load?                             Give it 10x your normal volume and check response quality
  ---------------------------- -------------------------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------------------------

**The Cautious Alpha Protocol**

This is the deployment philosophy: only ship features that have been proven through testing. Never deploy something because it seems to work. Deploy it because you have evidence it works.

-   Start with synthetic testing --- use fake data to test every scenario you can think of.

-   Require 95% confidence before real-world deployment. Not 80%. Not 90%. Ninety-five.

-   Deploy to a small group first. Watch closely. Measure everything.

-   Layer in more features as evidence accumulates, not as enthusiasm grows.

**\"Never Mess With Someone\'s Business on a Hunch\"**

This is the single most important principle in AI deployment. A business is someone\'s livelihood. Their mortgage, their children\'s meals, their employees\' wages --- all of it depends on that business working properly.

If you are not certain an AI feature helps, do not deploy it. Certainty comes from testing, not from optimism.

**10. RADICAL ATTRIBUTION GUIDE**

Attribution is not a legal requirement (though sometimes it is). It is a trust requirement. When you tell people where your ideas came from, they trust you more, not less. It shows you have done the research, you understand the landscape, and you are honest about what is yours and what is not.

**How to Properly Attribute Sources**

Every time you use a methodology, framework, or idea that originated with someone else, say so. It does not need to be complicated:

-   **Informal:** \"This approach is based on \[Name\]\'s work on \[Topic\].\"

-   **Formal:** \"\[Framework Name\] --- originated by \[Person\], \[Year\]. Applied here with adaptation for \[Context\].\"

-   **Comprehensive:** Use the full attribution schema below.

**The Attribution Schema**

For thorough attribution, track five layers:

  ------------ -------------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------
  **Layer**    **Question It Answers**                      **Example**
  Originator   Who created the foundational idea?           W. Edwards Deming --- PDCA cycle
  Formaliser   Who structured it into a usable framework?   Motorola / Bill Smith --- Six Sigma (1986)
  Builder      Who adapted it for this specific context?    Ewan Bramley --- AMPS for SMBs (2026)
  Tools        What tools or technologies enable it?        Claude AI, FalkorDB, Beast server
  Prior Art    What related work influenced it?             Toyota Production System, CMMI, V-Model
  ------------ -------------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------

**Why Attribution Builds Trust**

Behavioural psychology tells us that transparency increases credibility. Robert Cialdini\'s principle of authority states that people trust those who demonstrate knowledge --- and attribution demonstrates knowledge.

Paradoxically, admitting that an idea is not originally yours makes people more likely to trust the parts that are yours. It signals intellectual honesty, which is rare and valued.

In business terms: clients trust providers who say \"we built on Deming\'s work\" more than those who imply they invented continuous improvement from scratch.
