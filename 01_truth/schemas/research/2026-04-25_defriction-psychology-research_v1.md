PART ONE
The Lottery Winner Problem
Ewan's instinct — that radically improving someone's working life might backfire like a lottery win — was
partially right and partially wrong. The research tells a more nuanced story.
The Classic Study
The foundational research is Brickman, Coates & Janoff-Bulman (1978)[1]. They studied 22 lottery
winners, 22 controls, and 29 accident victims. The headline finding: lottery winners reported happiness at
4.0 out of 5, while controls reported 3.82 — a difference that was not statistically significant. More
tellingly, winners took less pleasure from mundane daily activities (socialising, eating breakfast, hearing a
joke) than controls did.
The Myth vs. the Reality
The widely cited claim that "70% of lottery winners go bankrupt" is actually a myth. As Forbes reports[2],
this statistic originated from a 2001 financial education symposium with no empirical backing. Modern
research paints a more nuanced picture — some winners thrive, others struggle, but the catastrophic
narrative is unsupported by data.
Hedonic Adaptation: The Real Mechanism
The underlying phenomenon is hedonic adaptation — the well-documented tendency for people to return
to a baseline level of happiness after positive (or negative) life changes. Lyubomirsky's Hedonic
Adaptation Prevention (HAP) model[3] identifies two routes by which adaptation erodes gains:
• Positive emotions fade over time — the initial thrill of improvement wears off
• Aspirations rise — yesterday's improvement becomes today's "new normal," and expectations shift
upward
Critically, the HAP model shows that adaptation is slower when people actively attend to the change —
when they remember how things used to be and appreciate the difference.
British panel data[4] confirms a related finding: lottery winnings do measurably improve mental health, but
winners also tend to smoke more and drink more socially, counteracting the health effects. The overall
health impact ends up being negligible.
KEY INSIGHT FOR AMPLIFIED
The lottery parallel is real but inverted. Lottery winners receive a passive windfall — money with no
effort. Defriction delivers an active capability gain — better processes that people helped build.
Research specifically shows that hedonic adaptation is slower when people attend to the historical
contingency of the change — i.e., when they remember how things were before. This is precisely
why progress tracking matters.

PART TWO
The Change Saturation Trap
The real danger isn't the lottery winner problem. It's change saturation — the point at which an
organisation's capacity to absorb change is overwhelmed.
The Scale of the Problem
Prosci research[5] reveals that 73% of organisations report being near, at, or past their change saturation
point. Among change-fatigued employees: 54% actively look for new roles, 48% report increased
tiredness and stress, and 37% trust their employer less.
The 70% Failure Myth
McKinsey's famous claim that "70% of change programmes fail" has become gospel. However, a
rigorous meta-review published in the Journal of Change Management[6] found no valid empirical
evidence for the 70% figure. A systematic review in the Journal of Change Management[7] confirmed this
— the statistic is a narrative, not a fact. Change is hard, but not that hard.
Emotions During Transformation
IE Insights data[8] reveals a critical asymmetry: during successful transformation, negative emotions
increase by 25%. During unsuccessful transformation, they increase by 130%. The differentiator is
empathy and coaching culture — BetterUp research shows employees in high-coaching cultures are 20%
more likely to recover from stressful experiences.
The Digital Transformation Trap
A 2025 study published in PLOS One[9] found that digital transformation can diminish employees' sense of
autonomy, reduce feelings of accomplishment, and increase social isolation. However, perceived
organisational support moderates this effect — when employees feel supported, the burnout effect is
significantly dampened.
The Sponge Metaphor
Change saturation works like a sponge: it can only absorb so much water before it's saturated and
everything runs off. Attempting to transform 176 processes simultaneously is a saturated sponge. But
making 0.5% improvement per day across a rolling subset — that's wringing the sponge between each
squeeze, maintaining absorptive capacity.

PART THREE
Identity, Agency & Learned Helplessness
Beyond saturation, there is a deeper psychological risk: when you change how someone works, you are
— at a fundamental level — changing who they are at work.
Identity Under Threat
Neurofied research[10] documents how organisational change challenges personal and professional
identity, creating a sense of disconnection. People who have built expertise and routines over years can
feel their competence is being invalidated — even when the change is objectively positive.
Learned Helplessness in Organisations
When employees repeatedly encounter situations where their actions produce no meaningful results, they
develop learned helplessness — they stop trying. Research indicates that organisations with high learned
helplessness generate 50–60% fewer innovative suggestions. In manufacturing contexts, helpless teams
produce 3–4x higher defect rates[11].
The GM NUMMI case study is instructive: the same workers who produced terrible quality in GM factories
went on to produce Toyota-level quality at the NUMMI joint venture — when given autonomy and
problem-solving authority. Learned helplessness can persist for decades until the environment changes.
Drivers of Resistance
A systematic review published in Frontiers in Psychology[12] identifies five primary drivers of resistance to
change: (a) fear of the unknown, (b) lack of trust, (c) loss of control, (d) poor communication, and (e)
habitual comfort. The same research found that organisational justice (fairness of process), perceived
organisational support, and leader–member exchange quality all mediate resistance. When employees
feel supported and treated fairly, readiness for change increases significantly.
KEY INSIGHT FOR AMPLIFIED
The #1 risk of defriction is that staff feel it's being done to them rather than with them. If Amplified
optimises their processes without their input, you create learned helplessness — "the computer
decided how I do my job." The antidote is co-creation. Staff must be the ones clicking "approve" on
each improvement, not just receiving it.

PART FOUR
Why Kaizen 0.5 Is the Right Medicine
The evidence strongly supports Amplified's chosen approach. Small, continuous improvement — Kaizen
— is not just a manufacturing philosophy; it's a psychologically optimal strategy for organisational
change.
The Compound Effect
James Clear's analysis of continuous improvement[13] demonstrates the maths: a 1% improvement per
day compounds to a 37x improvement over a year. Even at 0.5% per day, the compound curve is
dramatic — and critically, each day's change is too small to trigger resistance.
Optimize Before You Digitize
The Process Excellence Network[14] advocates a critical sequencing principle: "optimise before you
digitise." Digital transformations fail when technology is layered onto broken processes. Kaizen enables
organisations to identify, repair, and continuously improve processes before automation. FPT Software's
"digital Kaizen" approach launched modest initiatives in short timeframes rather than big-bang
transformation — with notably higher success rates.
Leadership & Psychological Safety
The Lean Blog[15] emphasises that Kaizen success depends on two factors: leadership engagement and
psychological safety. If the Study phase of Plan-Do-Study-Act shows that a change wasn't actually an
improvement, the team must feel safe to reverse it. Without psychological safety, people propose only
safe, incremental changes — which undermines the entire Kaizen philosophy.
The STEAM-ME Model for SMEs
Research published in Wiley[16] identified seven determinants of successful Kaizen specifically in SMEs —
the STEAM-ME model:
• Senior management support
• Training
• Environment
• Assessment
• Motivation
• Mindset (newly identified as crucial)
• Engagement of all members

PART FIVE
How to Present the Progress Data
Cognitive load theory and behavioural psychology offer clear guidance on how Amplified should present
process improvement data across its dashboard.
Miller's Law: The 7±2 Constraint
Miller's Law (1956)[17] established that working memory holds approximately 7±2 items. Showing 176 KPIs
simultaneously is cognitively impossible. The solution: chunk the 176 processes into 5–7 meaningful
categories — and show category-level progress as the default view.
Progressive Disclosure
Progressive disclosure[18] is the UX principle of showing only what's necessary at any given time. Like a
form wizard that reveals one step at a time rather than a 30-field form, Amplified's dashboard should
reveal detail on demand, not by default.
Visual Hierarchy
As Cluster Design notes[19]: "If everything is important, nothing is important." Distributing all KPIs with
equal visual weight is the worst decision for a dashboard. Use visual hierarchy — big numbers for the
headline insight, small text for supporting detail.
The Endowed Progress Effect
Nunes & Dreze (2006)[20] demonstrated a powerful effect: people given an artificial head start on a loyalty
card (2 out of 10 stamps pre-filled) were significantly more likely to complete the card than those starting
from 0 out of 8 — even though both groups needed the same 8 stamps. The reframing from "not yet
begun" to "already underway" increases persistence and decreases completion time.
The Goal Gradient Effect
Hull's Goal Gradient Effect (1932)[21] shows that motivation increases as people approach a goal. Practical
implication: break 176 processes into small achievable clusters, show each cluster's completion
percentage, and watch people accelerate as they approach each milestone.
Gamification: A Caution
Gamification has diminishing returns. Lattice research[22] warns it can bury helpful feedback and motivate
for the wrong reasons. Gamified work systems can negatively impact employee engagement and
well-being when they feel coercive. Avoid leaderboards comparing staff internally.

PART SIX
External Benchmarking: The Social
Comparison Tightrope
Comparing to other businesses is a powerful motivational tool — but it cuts both ways.
Social Comparison Theory
Festinger's Social Comparison Theory (1954)[23] established that people naturally evaluate themselves by
comparing to others. Upward comparison (to better performers) can trigger either benign envy —
motivation to improve — or malicious envy — hostility and undermining behaviour. The outcome depends
on context.
Psychological Availability
PMC research (2023)[24] identified the critical moderating variable: psychological availability. When
employees believe they have the resources and capability to close the gap with better performers,
upward comparison triggers benign envy (learning behaviour). When resources feel scarce, the same
comparison triggers malicious envy (social undermining).
The Benchmark Trap
As noted in LinkedIn analysis[25]: "Beating the benchmark doesn't mean we're doing well. It might just
mean we're slightly less bad." External benchmarks should provide context, not targets. Perceptyx
guidance[26] adds that the best benchmark is one that helps interpret data with clarity — and warns that
tying benchmarks to incentives encourages response bias. Focus on improvement over time.
Benchmark Recommendations
• Anonymous aggregate benchmarks only — "businesses like yours," not named competitors
• Compare to industry median, not top performers (prevents demotivation)
• Frame trajectory, not position: "You've moved from 34th percentile to 58th in 3 months" not "You're
still behind"
• Never compare individual staff to each other — only compare the business to external norms
• Use downward comparison sparingly but effectively: "Most businesses don't even measure this. You
already are."
